What anban means

அன்பு.Anban — the quiet, constant kind of love.

Not the kind you announce — the kind stirred into a meal instead. That's the word my grandmother used for cooking, and it's the word we still use for pickling.

Every jar that leaves our kitchen in Theni starts with produce bought ripe that morning from the Vaigai basin, spice ground fresh on a granite ammikallu, and enough patience to let flavor actually happen instead of rushing it with vinegar and heat.

No preservatives beyond salt, oil and time. No two batches taste identical — and we think that's exactly the point.

From orchard to jar

Five steps. No shortcuts.

01

Harvest

Mangoes, limes and gooseberries picked ripe from Theni orchards the same morning — never cold-stored.

02

Sun-cure

Washed and cut produce dries under open sun for a full day, drawing out moisture the old way.

03

Stone-grind

Chili, mustard and fenugreek are ground fresh on a granite ammikallu, never pre-milled or bought powdered.

04

Hand-mix

Spice paste is folded through by hand in batches small enough that one person can taste every jar.

05

Clay-seal

Pickles rest in sun-warmed clay jars so oil and spice settle properly before anything is bottled.

Why Theni

Where the Vaigai meets the hills.

Theni sits where the Vaigai river basin runs into the Western Ghats — red soil suited to chili and gooseberry on one side, cool hill air for pepper and cardamom on the other. We buy what's ripe within a day's drive, not what's cheapest by the container.

That's also why our jars never taste quite the same twice. A drier season means sharper mango; a good rain means sweeter garlic. We'd rather bottle that honestly than smooth it out with more sugar.
